What is an FPGA?

A Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) is an integrated circuit designed to be configured by a customer or a designer after manufacturing—hence "field-programmable." Unlike traditional chips with fixed hardware paths, FPGAs use a grid of configurable logic blocks (CLBs) and programmable interconnects.

The Origin and Inventor

The FPGA was pioneered by Ross Freeman, the co-founder of Xilinx, in 1984. Freeman’s vision was a "blank slate" of silicon that could be re-molded into any circuit. His invention, the XC2064, was the world’s first commercially viable FPGA.

Source: National Inventors Hall of Fame: Ross Freeman

Custom ASIC vs. Hardwired GPU

FPGAs are often described as "custom ASICs" (Application-Specific Integrated Circuits). While a traditional ASIC is hard-baked at the factory for one specific task, an FPGA provides the efficiency of an ASIC with the flexibility of software.

  • ASIC: Maximum performance, zero flexibility, extremely high development cost.
  • GPU: Fixed "hardwired" architecture optimized for parallel arithmetic but constrained by instruction fetch/decode cycles.
  • FPGA: Bespoke hardware paths created on-the-fly; low latency and massive throughput for specific data streams.

Software-Defined Hardware (SDH)

SDH refers to the ability to use high-level programming languages (like C++, OpenCL, or Python via Altera/Xilinx toolchains) to generate physical hardware circuits. This allows developers to treat hardware as dynamically as they do cloud software.

FPGA Architecture

Modern FPGA architecture consists of three primary components: Configurable Logic Blocks (CLBs), I/O Blocks, and Programmable Interconnects. Advanced chips also include DSP slices and Block RAM (BRAM).

FPGA for AI Inference

FPGAs deliver elastic AI inference by co-designing machine learning models with the hardware itself. By utilizing custom precision (INT8, FP16, or even binary/ternary weights), FPGAs eliminate the overhead of standard processors.

Beyond the GPU: Disaggregated Inference

The "GPU-only" mindset is shifting. In advanced LLM deployments, we see Disaggregated Inference:

  • FPGA for Prefill: Handling the heavy I/O and initial token processing with ultra-low latency.
  • Trainium/GPU for Decode: Scaling the autoregressive token generation.

This hybrid approach ensures that the "time to first token" is minimized while maintaining high throughput for the rest of the generation sequence.

Conclusion: An Open-Minded Outlook on AI

As the AI landscape evolves, the industry is realizing that AI inference does not mean "GPU only." While GPUs remain vital for training, the inference phase demands the cost-efficiency, deterministic latency, and hardware-level customization that only FPGAs can provide.

The future of AI is a unified architecture where reconfigurable hardware and system software work in tandem to solve the world's most complex computational challenges.
