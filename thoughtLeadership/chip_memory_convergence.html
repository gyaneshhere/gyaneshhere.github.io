The Vanishing Boundary:
When Memory Becomes Compute

For decades, we've treated memory and processing as separate domains. That era is ending. The line between the chip and the memory isn't just blurring—it's disappearing entirely.

GK

Gyanesh Kumar

Field Technologist, Amazon Web Services

01 • THE CRISIS

The Memory Wall Just Hit Us Back

Let me be direct: Major Chip Labs are building the most powerful compute engines in human history, and they're starving them to death. The latest GPU can execute 500 trillion operations per second, but it spends most of its time waiting. Waiting for data. Waiting for memory.

Here's a number that should terrify you: on a B200 GPU, HBM memory accounts for 45% of manufacturing cost. The entire chip costs $6,400 to manufacture. Two massive logic dies from TSMC? $900. Eight HBM3e stacks? $2,900. The memory is three times more expensive than the actual compute silicon.

The problem isn't new, we've been talking about the "memory wall" since the 1990s. What's changed is the scale of the disaster. During LLM inference, the arithmetic intensity is approximately 1 FLOP per byte. The H100's ridge point is 295. This means the GPU operates at 0.34% of peak compute during token generation. Adding more tensor cores does nothing. Only bandwidth helps.

High Bandwidth Memory changed the game in 2015 when AMD shipped the first HBM-equipped GPU. Instead of spreading memory chips across a board, they stacked them vertically and placed them millimeters from the processor.

But first, understand the fundamental constraint: a DRAM cell is one transistor and one capacitor. Charge on the cap = 1, no charge = 0. It's the simplest possible memory cell, which is why DRAM is so dense. But reading is slow—the RC time constant of the bitline limits speed, and each read drains the capacitor, requiring refresh every 64ms. DDR5 runs at ~50 GB/s per channel. Not enough for AI.

HBM solves this by going wide, not fast. Instead of pushing each pin to higher speeds, you make the bus HUGE. HBM3e runs a 1024-bit interface versus DDR5's 64-bit. To fit 1024 wires between memory and GPU, you can't use a PCB—the traces would be too long, the crosstalk too severe. So you put memory on a silicon interposer, millimeters from the GPU, connected through thousands of copper-filled through-silicon vias drilled through each stacked die.

Modest per-pin speed. Extreme bus width. Physical proximity. The formula: 8 stacks × 1024 bits × 9.2 Gbps = 8 TB/s on a B200.

But here's what most people miss: HBM wasn't just about faster memory. It was about proximity. About breaking down the barrier between compute and storage. Each generation brought them closer together, physically and architecturally.

Look at what Chip Labs have accomplished: up to 12 DRAM dies stacked vertically, connected through thousands of microscopic vias, sitting on a silicon interposer mere millimeters from the GPU die. The entire assembly—processor, memory, interconnect—fits in a package smaller than a smartphone.

But here's what makes this so expensive: each die is ground down to 30 micrometers— thinner than a human hair—then drilled with through-silicon vias, stacked up to 16 high, and tested at every layer. Yield is 20-30% lower than DDR5. And producing 1 GB of HBM consumes the wafer area of 3 GB of DDR5. Every HBM production ramp tightens the entire DRAM market.

The implications are staggering. Internal memory bandwidth inside an HBM stack exceeds what we can achieve externally by orders of magnitude. A single HBM3E stack delivers 1.2 TB/s. Eight stacks? You're looking at 8 TB/s of aggregate bandwidth to a single chip— with memory sitting less than 1 millimeter from the compute cores.

But bandwidth is only part of the story. Energy efficiency is where the real revolution lies. Moving data costs energy—far more energy than computing on it. A double-precision floating-point operation consumes roughly 20 picojoules. Fetching that data from external DRAM? That's 640 picojoules. You spend 32 times more energy moving the data than processing it.

HBM's tight integration slashes this energy cost dramatically. Shorter interconnects, lower voltage operation (down to 0.7V with HBM4), and massive parallelism through wide interfaces combine to deliver 60% energy reduction in real-world AI workloads.

05 • ECONOMICS

The Memory Supplier Won the AI Boom

Here's an uncomfortable truth about the AI revolution: SK Hynix controls 60% of HBM production and handles 70% of NVIDIA's orders. Their Q4 2025 operating margin? 58.4%— exceeding TSMC's 54%. The memory supplier captured more profit from the AI boom than the foundry.

This isn't just market dynamics—it's physics and economics colliding. HBM doesn't just solve a technical problem; it creates an entirely new cost structure for AI computing. When memory is 45% of your chip cost, the memory supplier becomes your most critical partner.

Micron learned this the hard way. They tried to design their HBM4 base die in-house using their DRAM process instead of TSMC's logic process. The result? The DRAM process couldn't route a 2048-bit interface or pass thermal specs. They lost essentially all orders for next-generation systems.

06 • THE PARADIGM SHIFT

Processing-in-Memory: The Final Convergence

Here's where it gets truly revolutionary. We stopped asking "how fast can we move data?" and started asking "why move it at all?"

Samsung's HBM-PIM, first demonstrated in 2021, embedded AI processing units directly inside the memory stack. Not near the memory. Not next to the memory. Inside it. Lightweight compute engines sitting at each bank boundary, capable of executing operations on data without shuttling it across the interposer to the GPU.

The results speak for themselves. In the Xilinx Alveo accelerator, HBM-PIM delivered 2.5× system performance improvement while cutting energy consumption by over 60%. For specific operations like matrix-vector multiplication—the backbone of neural networks— performance gains reached 8.9×.

And we're just getting started. The technology is expanding beyond HBM. Samsung's AXDIMM brings processing to standard DRAM modules. LPDDR5-PIM targets mobile devices with on-device AI. The principle is universal: move computation to where the data lives, not the other way around.

07 • NEXT FRONTIER

NVLink, GB200, and Unified Memory

The GB200 Superchip represents the next inflection point. Here's what we built: a Grace CPU and Blackwell GPU connected via NVLink-C2C at 1.8 TB/s bidirectional bandwidth, with hardware cache coherence treating their memories—LPDDR5X and HBM3e—as a unified address space.

Think of NVLink as the "memory highway with traffic cops." The bandwidth—1.8 TB/s—lets GPUs access CPU memory and vice versa as if it were local. But the real magic is the coherence protocol: hardware directories track cache-line ownership, issue invalidations, and snoop caches across chips. Zero software intervention. No explicit memcpy() calls. No CUDA migrations. Just direct load/store to peer memory.

This eliminates the PCIe bottleneck entirely. CPU RAM acts as a "shared L4 cache" for GPUs. For trillion-parameter models in NVL72 racks, this seamless memory sharing is critical. The result? Truly unified memory where the distinction between "CPU memory" and "GPU memory" becomes semantic rather than architectural.

And we're seeing similar convergence with CXL (Compute Express Link). CXL creates cache-coherent, unified address spaces spanning CPUs, GPUs, accelerators, and memory expanders. Research systems like CENT have demonstrated competitive LLM inference performance using CXL-connected PIM devices—GDDR6 with embedded processing units—networked through CXL switches, without traditional GPUs.

This is the future we're building: memory-centric computing where traditional processors orchestrate work but don't execute all of it. Where memory is active, intelligent, and capable. Where the distinction between "storage" and "compute" becomes semantic rather than architectural.

🔮 The Path Forward
08 • VISION

What Comes Next

The line isn't just disappearing—it's being deliberately erased. HBM4 is the watershed moment: it doubles the bus to 2048 bits and, for the first time, moves the base die to TSMC's logic process. This isn't just faster memory—it's custom silicon at the bottom of the memory stack: advanced ECC, memory controllers, and potentially in-memory compute.

Here's what we're building in our labs right now, what you'll see shipping in the next 2-5 years:

🔬 Custom Base Dies (HBM4)

Instead of generic memory controllers, we're embedding domain-specific accelerators— decompression engines, encryption units, data transformation pipelines—directly in the memory stack. The base die becomes programmable logic, not passive wiring.

🧠 Compute-Under-Memory

Processing units literally underneath the DRAM arrays, exploiting internal bandwidth that's 10× higher than what we expose externally. Matrix multiplication, attention mechanisms, convolutions—all happening where the data already sits.

🌐 Optical Interconnects

Through-silicon optical vias replacing electrical connections. Terabit-per-second bandwidth densities with sub-100 femtojoule-per-bit energy efficiency. The bandwidth wall becomes irrelevant.

🔄 Hybrid Memory Cubes

Mixing DRAM, SRAM, and emerging non-volatile technologies in a single stack. Each layer optimized for different workload characteristics—capacity, speed, persistence—all transparently managed.

🎯 Chiplet Ecosystems

Standardized interfaces allowing mix-and-match integration. GPU chiplets from one vendor, memory from another, interconnect IP from a third. UCIe and other emerging standards make heterogeneous integration routine.

⚡ Active Interposers

Silicon interposers evolving from passive wiring layers to active compute substrates. Routing intelligence, power management, even lightweight processing—all happening in what used to be just a connection layer.

09 • CONCLUSION

The End of an Era

We're witnessing the collapse of the Von Neumann architecture—the separation of processing and memory that has defined computing for 80 years. It's not happening because of some ideological shift or architectural fashion. It's happening because physics demands it.

The data is undeniable: moving data costs 32 times more energy than computing on it. A double-precision floating-point operation consumes 20 picojoules. Fetching that data from external DRAM? 640 picojoules. In our B200, we reduced cache-miss latency by 58% and cut memory stalls by 40-60% simply by making memory behave like L3 cache instead of external DRAM.

Distance has become the enemy. The only solution is to eliminate the distance—to fuse memory and compute into a unified whole. HBM3e sits less than 1mm from compute cores. NVLink-C2C delivers 1.8 TB/s of cache-coherent bandwidth between CPU and GPU. HBM4's custom base dies will embed compute logic directly in the memory stack.

This convergence isn't optional. Every meaningful advance in AI, in high-performance computing, in data-intensive workloads of any kind, depends on solving the memory bottleneck. And the only way to solve it is to stop treating memory as separate from computation.

"The future doesn't have chips connected to memory. It has memory that computes and compute that stores. The line between them won't blur—it will vanish."

We're not preparing for this future. We're building it. Every tape-out, every new architecture, every innovation in packaging and interconnect technology brings us closer to a world where the question "is this a processor or is this memory?" becomes meaningless.

The economics tell the story: when memory suppliers capture higher margins than foundries, when HBM costs three times more than the compute silicon it serves, when SK Hynix's 58.4% operating margin exceeds TSMC's 54%—the revolution is already here. The memory supplier won the AI boom because memory IS the bottleneck.

The line is disappearing. And what emerges on the other side will be more powerful than anything we've built before.
